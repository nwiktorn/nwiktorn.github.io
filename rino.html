A / Brief

A solid product.
Buried by its own packaging.

RINO had earned its reputation the hard way — product quality that held up in the field, user loyalty that didn't need marketing to survive. But the visual identity told a different story. Outdated label design, no clear brand logic, no system that could scale. Walk past it on a shelf and you'd assume it was the budget option.

The brief was deceptively simple: make RINO look like it deserves its shelf space. But what it really meant was more complicated — build a visual identity from scratch that could carry a full product line, hold up at centimetre scale on a label and at two-metre scale on a trade show banner, and actually survive contact with a printing press without falling apart.

No templates. No mood-board wallpaper. A real brand system that works in the real world.

C / Design system

One direction.
No hedge-betting.

The visual direction was built around a single tension: industrial authority meets precision engineering. Black as the base — not because it's safe, but because RINO earns it. A grid-locked logomark that reads instantly at any scale. Typography that doesn't apologise for itself.

Every label follows the same structural logic: category colour coded, hierarchy locked, production margins baked in. A new SKU doesn't require a redesign — it just slots into the system. That's not an accident. That's the whole point.

Packaging that earned its shelf space — not borrowed it.

Detail work

Labels that survive the product, not just the pitch deck.

Prepress wasn't an afterthought — it was part of the brief. Labels go on packaging that gets handled, stored, stacked and shipped. Every file left my hands print-ready: bleeds, spot colours where needed, correct profiles. Zero calls from the vendor asking basic questions. That's the standard.

D / Results & outcomes

The rebrand landed.
Everything that follows is easier now.

RINO now looks like a premium line — because it is one. The system is consistent, scalable and ready for whatever comes next. New products don't require new thinking. New markets don't require a redesign. The brand just grows into the space the guidelines already made room for.

More importantly: it passed the shelf test. Pick it up, read the label in three seconds, put it back knowing exactly what you held. That's the brief answered.
